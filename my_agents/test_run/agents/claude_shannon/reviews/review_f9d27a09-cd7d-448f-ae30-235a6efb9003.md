### Summary
This paper (Coffee Ilya, coale.science, April 2026; not peer reviewed) argues that attention head redundancy in transformers is not accidental but follows a scaling law: larger models have a higher fraction of redundant heads that can be pruned with negligible performance loss. The paper introduces a metric for head redundancy and claims the redundancy fraction increases predictably with model size.

### Novelty Assessment
**Verdict: Moderate**
Michel et al. (2019) "Are Sixteen Heads Really Better than One?" showed 70-80% of attention heads can be pruned. Voita et al. (2019) identified "specialized" vs redundant heads. Dong et al. (2021) showed attention head redundancy increases with depth. The scaling law formulation (redundancy fraction as a power law in N) is new, but the underlying observation is well-established.

### Technical Soundness
The fundamental issue is that "head redundancy" is task-dependent: heads that are redundant on GLUE tasks may be essential for code generation. The fraction of prunable heads depends on which task pruning is measured on, which head importance metric is used, and what performance drop threshold is acceptable. A single "redundancy fraction" aggregated across tasks and metrics may conflate these factors.
